Common Types of Banana Pests

Nematodes
They cause rotting of the corms and act as a vector to the fungus Fusarium oxysporum. Commercial farmers apply nematicides, which when properly applied, will protect the crop. Otherwise, the soil has to be cleared, plowed, and then exposed to the sun and left fallow for up to three years.
Data: Gardening Know How
Weevils
The black weevil (Cosmopolites sordidus) or banana stalk borer, banana weevil borer, or corm weevil is the second most destructive pest. Black weevils attack the base of the pseudostem and tunnel upward whereupon a jelly-like sap oozes out from the entry point.
Data: Gardening Know How
Thrips
Banana rust thrips (C. signipennis), as its name suggests, stains the peel, causing it to split and exposes the flesh which then begins to rot. Insecticidal dust (Diazinon) or a spraying of Dieldrin can control thrips, which pupate in the soil.
Data: Gardening Know How
Scarring beetle
The banana fruit scarring beetle, or coquito, invades the bunches when the fruit is young. The banana scab moth infests the inflorescence and is controlled with the use of an injection or dusting of pesticide.
Data: Gardening Know How
Banana aphid
Small reddish-brown to almost black oval shaped aphids. Aphids have two characteristic projections from the rear of the abdomen. Colonies can also be found in the unfurled top leaves of young plants and suckers or under leaf bases near ground level. Winged forms have prominent forewings with dark pigmented veins.
Data: Dept. of Agriculture and Fisheries, Queensland, Australia
Banana fruit caterpillar
Adults are medium to large moths measuring 50-60 mm across the wings. The darker forewings are dull grey-brown in colour with a dark brown V-shaped area on the fore margins. The hind wings are uniform light brown-grey.
Data: Dept. of Agriculture and Fisheries, Queensland, Australia
